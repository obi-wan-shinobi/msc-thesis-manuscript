\chapter{\label{cha:discussion}Discussion}

\section{Overall answer to the main research question}
\label{sec:discussion-main-question}

The main research question asked to what extent the Fourier decomposition of the
continuum infinite-width NTK on \(S^1\) persists under finite-sample and
finite-width perturbations.

In the continuum infinite-width setting, the Fourier decomposition is exact.
Proposition~\ref{prop:results-continuum-ntk} shows that the limiting NTK is
rotation-invariant on \(S^1\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:results-continuum-spectrum}
gives its Fourier eigenvalues explicitly. Since the limiting operator is a
convolution operator, the Fourier modes are exact eigenfunctions, and the
residual coefficients decay independently according to
\eqref{eq:results-continuum-gradient-flow-mode-decay}. This is the standard NTK
mechanism in an explicit form: in the infinite-width regime, training is
governed by a fixed kernel operator, and components aligned with larger
eigenvalues are learned faster
\citep{jacot2018ntk,lee2020wide,cao2020understandingspectralbiasdeep}. In
rotationally symmetric settings with uniform measure, this spectral ordering
becomes a literal frequency ordering in harmonic coordinates
\citep{bietti2019inductivebiasneuraltangent,basri2019convergence,basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks}.

The finite-sample results show that this Fourier description survives random
sampling on fixed low-frequency blocks. Theorem~\ref{thm:results-finite-sample-concentration}
controls the sampled Fourier geometry, the compressed sampled operator, and the
sampled eigen-action error. Figures~\ref{fig:results-finite-sample-diagnostics},
\ref{fig:results-random-sampling-first200}, and
\ref{fig:results-random-sampling-highfreq} show that these perturbations
decrease with sample size and that the diagonal prediction still captures the
dominant early-time residual dynamics. In this sense, the thesis complements
prior NTK spectral-bias work, which mainly studies the population operator and
its eigenfunctions, by quantifying how a finite random sample perturbs that
operator on a fixed Fourier block
\citep{basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks,bowman2022spectralbiasoutsidetraining,cao2020understandingspectralbiasdeep}.

At finite width, the current result concerns the frozen tangent operator at
initialization. Theorem~\ref{thm:results-finite-width-frozen-concentration}
shows that, for fixed \(K\), the finite-width block \(B_m^{(K)}\) is centered at
the continuum block \(\Lambda_K\) and concentrates around it as \(m\) increases.
Figure~\ref{fig:results-finite-width-concentration} confirms this trend
empirically. Thus finite width introduces a random perturbation of the continuum
Fourier operator, and this perturbation decreases with width. This should be
read as a frozen-kernel baseline rather than a complete finite-width theory,
which is consistent with the broader literature showing that finite-width kernel
fluctuations and kernel evolution can matter during training
\citep{bordelon2023dynamicsfinitewidthkernel,vyas2022limitationsntkunderstandinggeneralization,golikov2022neuraltangentkernelsurvey}.

Thus the answer is conditional. The Fourier picture is exact for the continuum
infinite-width operator. It is stable under finite sampling only on fixed
low-frequency blocks, and it is stable at finite width only for the frozen
tangent operator at initialization. The thesis therefore establishes a controlled
perturbation theory around the ideal NTK model, not a complete theory of
finite-width training. The missing case is the evolving finite-width tangent
operator, where the kernel can change during training and the Fourier block
structure need not remain fixed.

\section{Q1: finite-sample changes}
\label{sec:discussion-q1}

Q1 asked how replacing the uniform measure on \(S^1\) by \(n\) sampled points
changes the action of the infinite-width NTK on a fixed low-frequency Fourier
subspace, and what this implies for residual dynamics.

\subsection{Sampled Fourier structure}
\label{subsec:discussion-q1-structure}

The finite-sample theory separates the effect of sampling into three objects.
The matrix \(G\) measures whether the sampled Fourier basis remains close to
orthonormal. The matrix \(H\) measures the sampled operator in Fourier
coordinates. The quantity \(A\Phi-\Phi\Lambda^{(n)}\) measures the part of the
sampled operator action that is not captured by the diagonal prediction.

Theorem~\ref{thm:results-finite-sample-concentration} shows that all three
quantities are controlled for fixed \(K\). Figure~\ref{fig:results-finite-sample-diagnostics}
supports this numerically: the errors decrease as \(n\) increases. This places
the sampled problem between two settings already emphasised in the literature:
the exact population operator under a uniform distribution, where harmonic modes
diagonalize the NTK, and the density-weighted population operator under
non-uniform sampling, where the relevant eigendirections become
distribution-dependent
\citep{basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks,bietti2019inductivebiasneuraltangent,cao2020understandingspectralbiasdeep}.
What is added here is an explicit perturbation statement for the randomly
sampled operator on a prescribed Fourier block.

\subsection{Residual dynamics and preconditioning}
\label{subsec:discussion-q1-dynamics}

The sampled dynamics experiments show how this approximate structure affects
training. In Figure~\ref{fig:results-random-sampling-first200}, the observed
residual mode amplitudes closely follow the diagonal prediction from
\(\lambda_p^{(n)}\) during early training. This shows that the main rate ordering
is still explained by the finite-sample corrected eigenvalues. At later times,
deviations become more visible, as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:results-random-sampling-highfreq}, which is consistent with the
decomposition
\[
	A\Phi
	=
	\Phi\Lambda^{(n)}
	+
	\bigl(A\Phi-\Phi\Lambda^{(n)}\bigr).
\]
The second term is small, but after the diagonal dynamics has reduced some
components by several orders of magnitude, its effect can become visible.

The preconditioning experiment supports the same interpretation from another
angle. The theory preconditioner uses only the diagonal prediction
\(\Lambda^{(n)}\), while the empirical preconditioner uses the observed block
\(C=G^{-1}H\). Figure~\ref{fig:results-preconditioned-mode-decay} shows that the
theory preconditioner already removes most of the rate separation on the
retained block, while Figure~\ref{fig:results-preconditioner-gap} shows that the
difference between the theory and empirical constructions decreases with \(n\).
This is useful primarily as a diagnostic: it indicates that most of the sampled
spectral bias on the retained block is explained by the predicted eigenvalue
scaling, with the remaining discrepancy caused by finite-sample mixing between
the retained modes.

\subsection{Answer to Q1}
\label{subsec:discussion-answer-q1}

The answer to Q1 is that finite sampling does not destroy the Fourier
description, but it changes its status. The Fourier modes are no longer exact
eigenvectors of the sampled operator. Instead, on a fixed retained block, the
sampled Fourier basis remains close to orthonormal, the compressed operator
remains close to the finite-sample diagonal prediction, and the off-diagonal
eigen-action error decreases with \(n\). This explains why the early residual
dynamics still follows the predicted eigenvalue ordering, while later deviations
appear once small residual components become comparable to the sampling error.

\section{Q2: finite-width changes}
\label{sec:discussion-q2}

Q2 asked how finite width changes the continuum operator and the residual
dynamics when the input measure remains uniform on \(S^1\).

\subsection{Frozen tangent operator as the current baseline}
\label{subsec:discussion-q2-frozen}

The current finite-width result addresses the frozen tangent operator at
initialization. Theorem~\ref{thm:results-finite-width-frozen-concentration}
shows that
\[
	\mathbb E[B_m^{(K)}]=\Lambda_K
\]
and that \(B_m^{(K)}\) concentrates around \(\Lambda_K\) as \(m\) increases.
Thus, on a fixed low-frequency block, finite width acts as a random perturbation
of the continuum Fourier operator.

Figure~\ref{fig:results-finite-width-concentration} supports this conclusion.
The median and \(95\%\) quantile of
\(\|B_m^{(K)}-\Lambda_K\|_{\max}\) both decrease as \(m\) increases. The
theoretical envelope is conservative, but the qualitative trend matches the
concentration statement. This agrees with the role of the infinite-width NTK as
a useful baseline, while also showing why finite-width fluctuations can matter
quantitatively
\citep{jacot2018ntk,lee2020wide,bordelon2023dynamicsfinitewidthkernel,vyas2022limitationsntkunderstandinggeneralization}.

\subsection{High-frequency sensitivity}
\label{subsec:discussion-q2-relative}

The finite-width perturbation should be judged relative to the spectral scale of
the modes being studied. Let
\[
	E_m^{(K)} := B_m^{(K)}-\Lambda_K .
\]
For residual decay on an active frequency block \(S\), an absolute bound on
\(\|E_m^{(K)}\|\) is not by itself enough. The perturbation is dynamically small
only when its size is small compared with the eigenvalues governing the decay of
the active modes, for instance when
\[
	\|E_m^{(K)}\|_{\mathrm{op}}
	\ll
	\lambda_{\min,S},
	\qquad
	\lambda_{\min,S}:=\min_{k\in S}\lambda_k .
\]
For eigenspace stability, the relevant comparison is instead with the spectral
gap separating a retained frequency block from neighbouring blocks:
\[
	\|E_m^{(K)}\|_{\mathrm{op}}
	\ll
	\operatorname{gap}(S),
	\qquad
	\operatorname{gap}(S)
	:=
	\min_{k\in S,\ell\notin S}|\lambda_k-\lambda_\ell| .
\]
This distinction matters because the cosine and sine modes at the same
frequency form a degenerate two-dimensional eigenspace. The stable object is
therefore the frequency subspace, not an individual sampled cosine or sine
vector.

Since both the eigenvalues and the gaps decrease at high frequency, a fixed
absolute perturbation has a larger relative effect on high-frequency blocks than
on low-frequency blocks. This gives a more precise version of the usual
spectral-bias interpretation. In the continuum model, high frequencies decay
slowly because their eigenvalues are small. Under finite sampling or finite
width, those same small spectral scales also make the high-frequency Fourier
structure less robust to perturbations. This is consistent with empirical
observations that high-frequency components are learned later and are more
sensitive to data distribution and perturbations
\citep{rahaman2019spectralbiasneuralnetworks,basri2019convergence,basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks}.

\subsection{Answer to Q2}
\label{subsec:discussion-answer-q2}

The current answer to Q2 is partial. For the frozen tangent operator, finite
width preserves the continuum Fourier block up to a random perturbation that
decreases with \(m\). The full answer requires a comparison between frozen and
evolving finite-width dynamics.

\section{Role of the trainable bias}
\label{sec:discussion-bias}

The trainable hidden bias is not a cosmetic modelling choice in this problem.
Corollary~\ref{cor:results-bias-spectrum} shows that the bias term changes the
nullspace of the continuum operator: without it, the odd modes \(k\geq 3\) have
zero eigenvalue, while with it they acquire nonzero but small eigenvalues.
Therefore the bias determines whether these modes can be learned at all in the
fixed-kernel continuum dynamics.

This agrees with earlier harmonic analyses of two-layer ReLU networks on the
sphere and circle, where bias-free models fail to capture odd harmonics beyond
the first mode
\citep{basri2019convergence,basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks}. The advantage
of the present calculation is that, in this normalization, the effect is visible
directly at the level of the kernel and its Fourier eigenvalues. Thus
Figure~\ref{fig:results-continuum-odd-modes} does not only illustrate slower
odd-mode learning; it distinguishes modes that are present with small
eigenvalues from modes that are absent because the corresponding eigenvalues
vanish.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}

\subsection{Scope of the concentration results}
\label{subsec:discussion-limitations-concentration}

The concentration results should be read as fixed-block perturbation results.
They control a prescribed low-frequency space \(\mathcal H_K\) with fixed \(K\).
They do not give a uniform statement over all Fourier modes, and the bounds are
not designed to remain sharp as \(K\) grows with \(n\) or \(m\). This is important
because the high-frequency eigenvalues and eigengaps become small, so controlling
higher and higher frequencies would require increasingly accurate operator
approximations.

The numerical constants are also conservative. In the finite-sample theorem, the
proof controls entries of the relevant matrices and then applies union bounds
over the retained block. The bound for \(H-\Lambda^{(n)}\) uses a
bounded-differences argument, and the bound for
\(A\Phi-\Phi\Lambda^{(n)}\) uses a conditional Bernstein estimate followed by a
union bound over sample-mode pairs. In the finite-width theorem, each projected
entry is controlled using a coarse tail envelope before another union bound is
applied. These steps are sufficient to prove concentration, but they discard
matrix structure and variance information. This explains why the theoretical
envelopes in Figures~\ref{fig:results-finite-sample-diagnostics} and
\ref{fig:results-finite-width-concentration} are much larger than the observed
errors.

A sharper analysis would need to use the structure of the objects being
controlled. For finite sampling, \(H\) is a bounded second-order statistic, so
concentration tools for U- or V-statistics may give tighter estimates. For both
finite sampling and finite width, direct matrix concentration in operator norm
would be preferable to entrywise control followed by norm conversion.

\subsection{What is not proved about dynamics}
\label{subsec:discussion-limitations-dynamics}

The finite-sample dynamics experiments support the diagonal prediction, but the
theory does not yet give a complete time-uniform residual bound. The experiments
show that the predicted rates explain the dominant early-time behaviour. At late
times, however, some residual components have already become very small, so even
a small off-diagonal error can become visible in relative terms. The current
bounds identify the source of this effect, but they do not give a sharp
description of the full residual trajectory for all times.

The preconditioning experiment should also be interpreted as a diagnostic rather
than as a proposed training method. The theory preconditioner tests whether the
observed rate separation is mainly explained by the diagonal eigenvalue scaling.
The empirical preconditioner tests how much is gained by using the actual sampled
low-frequency block. These experiments support the spectral interpretation, but
they do not by themselves establish a general-purpose preconditioning algorithm
or a generalization guarantee.

\subsection{Frozen versus evolving finite-width kernels}
\label{subsec:discussion-limitations-evolving}

The finite-width theorem concerns the tangent operator at initialization. It does
not control the operator after training begins. Therefore it separates one source
of finite-width error, namely random initialization of the tangent kernel, but
not the second source, namely kernel evolution during training. A full answer to
Q2 would require a time-dependent comparison between the frozen block
\(B_m^{(K)}\), the evolving block \(C_t^{(K)}\), and the continuum block
\(\Lambda_K\). Without such a comparison, the finite-width part of the thesis
should be read as a frozen-kernel baseline.

There is also no joint theorem combining finite sampling, finite width, and
training time. The finite-sample analysis studies the infinite-width sampled
operator, while the finite-width analysis studies the continuum projected frozen
operator. In experiments these effects occur together. A complete perturbation
theory would need to control both simultaneously.

\subsection{Model assumptions}
\label{subsec:discussion-limitations-model}

The analysis relies on the special structure of \(S^1\) with the uniform measure.
In this setting the limiting kernel is rotation-invariant, hence a convolution
operator, and the Fourier basis diagonalizes it exactly. If the input
distribution is non-uniform, the integral operator is no longer diagonal in the
global Fourier basis; its eigenfunctions become distribution-dependent
\citep{basri2020frequencybiasneuralnetworks}. If the input domain is changed,
the appropriate harmonic basis also changes or may no longer be available in
closed form.

The architecture and scaling assumptions are also part of the result. The thesis
uses a two-layer ReLU network in NTK scaling. Changing the activation, adding or
removing bias terms, changing the parameterization, or moving to a feature-
learning scaling changes the limiting kernel and therefore changes the spectrum
that drives the dynamics. The trainable bias is especially important in this
model because it changes the odd-frequency part of the spectrum. Thus the
Fourier conclusions should be understood as conclusions about this explicit NTK
model, not as architecture-independent statements about neural networks in
general.
