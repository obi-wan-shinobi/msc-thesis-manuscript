\chapter{\label{cha:conclusion}Conclusion and future work}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis studied spectral bias in an exactly solvable NTK setting: a
two-layer ReLU network on the unit circle \(S^1\). In the continuum
infinite-width limit with the uniform measure, the NTK is rotation-invariant and
therefore diagonal in the Fourier basis. This gives an explicit fixed-kernel
description of training: each Fourier component of the residual decays
independently, at a rate determined by the corresponding kernel eigenvalue.

The main contribution is to analyze how this ideal Fourier picture changes when
the two idealizations are relaxed. For Q1, finite sampling replaces exact
Fourier diagonalization by approximate diagonalization on fixed low-frequency
blocks. The sampled Fourier Gram matrix, the compressed sampled operator, and
the sampled eigen-action error all concentrate as the sample size increases.
This explains why the finite-sample corrected eigenvalues predict the dominant
early residual dynamics, and why the theory-based preconditioner removes most
of the observed low-frequency rate separation.

For Q2, the thesis establishes the corresponding frozen finite-width baseline.
On each fixed low-frequency block, the finite-width tangent operator at
initialization is centered at the continuum Fourier block and concentrates
around it as the width increases. Thus finite width acts, at initialization, as
a random perturbation of the continuum NTK spectrum. The thesis does not yet
prove the analogous statement for the evolving tangent kernel during training.

The central lesson is that high-frequency modes are fragile in two related
ways. In the continuum model they are learned slowly because their eigenvalues
are small. Under finite sampling or finite width, the same small eigenvalues and
small eigengaps make high-frequency blocks more sensitive to perturbations.
Thus the thesis refines the spectral-bias picture in this model: high
frequencies are not only slower to learn, but also less stable under the
perturbations introduced by sampling and finite width.

\section{Future work}
\label{sec:future-work}

The first priority is to complete the finite-width part of the story. The next
step is to compare the frozen tangent block \(B_m^{(K)}\) with the evolving
block \(C_t^{(K)}\) during training. This would separate finite-width effects
caused by random initialization from effects caused by feature learning or
kernel evolution.

A second direction is to prove a joint perturbation result that includes finite
sampling, finite width, and training time in one statement. The present thesis
studies these effects separately. In actual finite-width experiments, however,
the sampled operator, the random initialization of the tangent kernel, and the
time-dependence of the kernel all interact.

A third direction is to sharpen the concentration theory. The current bounds
use entrywise concentration, coarse tail estimates, and union bounds. They prove
the correct qualitative behaviour but are too conservative as numerical
predictions. Direct operator-norm bounds, variance-sensitive estimates, or
concentration tools for U- and V-statistics may give bounds closer to the
observed errors.

Finally, the analysis should be extended beyond the uniform circle model.
Non-uniform input densities, higher-dimensional spheres, other activations, and
different parameterizations all change the relevant kernel spectrum. Studying
these settings would clarify which conclusions are specific to the exactly
diagonal Fourier model on \(S^1\), and which reflect a more general mechanism
behind spectral bias.
